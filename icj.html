The International Court of Justice (ICJ)

What is ICJ?

The International Court of Justice (ICJ) is the UN’s principal judicial body, resolving legal disputes between states and offering advisory opinions. Advocates present legal arguments based on international law, while judges evaluate these arguments to issue a ruling.

TOPIC:
The legal status of Taiwan

Taiwan's status is disputed, with China claiming it as part of its territory while Taiwan operates independently. This topic explores international recognition, diplomatic challenges, and the implications for global relations.

Advocate List

Agents for Applicant (Taiwan): arguing for Taiwan’s legal status as a state, providing evidence, legal arguments and precedents. Agents for Respondents (China): arguing for sovereignty over Taiwan, and arguing against Taiwan’s independence and sovereignty. Witnesses/Experts: provide testimony on issues like historical claims, treaties, and international recognition. (International law experts, diplomats of countries with relations with taiwan/china: Taiwan’s minister of foreign affairs, PM of Japan, China’s minister of foreign affairs, international law expert Dr. Wang Tieya, ICJ Judge Xue Hanqin) Judges 4-6: will listen to both sides, including testimonies of experts, ask questions and deliberate, and ultimately decide on the legal status of Taiwan. They do NOT decide on the procedure of the committee—that is for the impartial chairs, who will oversee the case & choose best agent/judge/expert, honorable mention, shoutouts.
